the typewriter

Among the many assertions of who was responsible for creating the first typewriter, Englishman Henry Mill was one of the first. Not yet titled “typewriter” but rather “Machine for Transcribing Letters”, his patent -- dated in 1714 -- described a device that was intended to transcribe letters permanently and neatly, and therefore might be “of great use in settlements and publick records”. It is believed that over one hundred prototypes were created from inventors ranging from countries such as Italy to Denmark. However, there is no arguing the fact that Christopher Latham Sholes was the first to assemble a successful typewriter ( "Computer Keyboard").

... an artificial machine or method for impressing or transcribing of letters, one after another, as in writing, whereby all writing whatsoever may be engrossed in paper or parchment so neat and exact as not to be distinguished from print; that the said machine or method may be of great use in settlements and publick records, the impression being deeper and more lasting than any other writing, and not to be erased or counterfeited without manifest discovery. -- henry mill's patent

Influenced by the earlier prototypes of his day, Sholes collaborated with a few of his friends -- Carlos Glidden and Samuel Soule -- to invent the typewriter in 1867. This model remains on display at the Smithsonian National Museum of American History. Despite Sholes’s attempts to constantly change and refine his product over the next several years, he was having difficulty selling it on his own. Finally, he sold his patents for $12,000 to two entrepreneurs, James Densmore and George Yost, who were able to persuade a manufacturing company in New York. This company, titled E. Remington and Sons, began to mass produce typewriters along with their “fire arms, sewing machines and farm machinery” (“Typewriter History”) in 1873.

Officially released in 1874, Remington titled the first model the Sholes and Gliden Type-Writer. Two mechanics from the sewing machine division at the company took on the task of engineering it; mostly made out of wood, it included a foot-pedal for the carriage return, and flowery details for decoration (“Typewriter History”). It introduced the QWERTY keyboard, which was adapted so that the “mechanical rods attached to the keys would [not] stick to each other” when people typed too quickly (D’Cruz). The mechanics of the machine, however, did not allow for the typist to see what they were writing. Similar to many first models of other inventions, it suffered from other imperfections in the engineering and required many adjustments.

Mechanical issues were not the only reason that typewriters struggled to be accepted. Not only was the price high for a floundering economy, but many believed that they would be impractical or that typing appeared impersonal to customers. After further adjusting the machine and adding the shift key for both lower and uppercase letters, the second model of the Remington took off within the next decade. The era of industrialization and the discovery of employees who were in need of a way to efficiently record accounts and send correspondence helped boost its popularity (“Typewriter History”).

Soon after its release, the second model was adjusted so that individuals did not have to manually lift the platen to see their content (“Typewriter History”). This was a major upgrade and allowed for even greater convenience, further promoting the typewriter at a time where everyone believed it could not possibly improved any more. Of course, as the years continued, small adjustments were made, many of them stylistic; as typewriters began creeping into households, companies started designing machines that were sleeker and more colorful. The 1930s jump started a movement of not only marketing towards housewives and children, but also marked the year that portable and electrical versions were introduced (Dugan).

Throughout the 1940s until the 1960s, typewriters remained the “main means of data entry”; however, it faced heavy competition as personal computers started emerging and competing ( "Computer Keyboard"). Even the IBM Selectric, which eliminated the issue of typewriter jams, could no longer compete with the efficiency of the challenger. Although it was slowly phased out in favor of the computer, a fair percentage of the population today keep typewriters in their possession as keepsakes (“Typing Through Time: Keyboard History”).
